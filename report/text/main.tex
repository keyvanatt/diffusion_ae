\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

The numerical simulation of physical systems governed by partial differential equations (PDEs) underpins a vast range of scientific and engineering disciplines, from computational fluid dynamics and structural mechanics to atmospheric modelling and chemical process engineering. High-fidelity solvers—whether based on finite elements, finite volumes, or spectral methods—can capture complex multi-scale phenomena with great accuracy, but at a computational cost that renders them impractical for applications requiring thousands or millions of forward evaluations. Real-time decision making, Bayesian inference over large parameter spaces, optimal experimental design, and digital twin frameworks all fall into this category: each query to the solver may take minutes to hours, making exhaustive exploration computationally intractable \cite{ammar2024hybrid,quarteroni2025}.

This tension between accuracy and computational cost has motivated a rapidly growing body of work on \emph{surrogate modelling} (also referred to as emulation or metamodelling), in which a cheaper approximate model replaces the high-fidelity solver for a given parameter range. Classical surrogate approaches, such as Gaussian processes or polynomial chaos expansions, offer rigorous uncertainty estimates but scale poorly with input dimensionality and struggle to capture the high-dimensional structure of field-valued outputs. Data-driven methods based on deep learning have emerged as a compelling alternative, offering both the flexibility to represent complex non-linear maps and the ability to produce full spatial fields as outputs \cite{karniadakis2021,quarteroni2025}.

A particularly productive paradigm in this context is \emph{physics-informed machine learning}, which seeks to embed physical knowledge—whether through governing equations, symmetry constraints, or conservation laws—directly into the learning architecture or loss function \cite{karniadakis2021,meng2021survey}. The present work draws on this philosophy by leveraging the structure of the Laplace transform as a physically motivated temporal basis, decomposing transient fields into independent frequency components that are individually amenable to surrogate modelling.

\textbf{Application context.} The motivating application is the emulation of atmospheric dispersion models for pollutant concentration prediction, including methane (CH4) \cite{fan2024,guo2022}. Replacing such simulations with fast neural surrogates accelerates real-time monitoring and inverse source identification.

\textbf{Central contribution.} The high dimensionality of field-valued outputs—up to $N_t \times N^2$ degrees of freedom per simulation—makes direct regression intractable. The central approach is to first learn a compact latent representation of the solution fields, either through linear dimensionality reduction (POD, SVD) or through non-linear autoencoders, and then train a surrogate that maps physical parameters $\vtheta$ into this latent space. This decoupling reduces the surrogate output dimensionality by orders of magnitude while allowing each component to be trained and validated independently.

\textbf{Outline.} Section~\ref{sec:stationary} introduces the framework on a stationary 2-D convection-diffusion problem. Section~\ref{sec:transient} presents the transient CH4 problem, comparing linear SVD-based latent representations against a non-linear frequency-conditioned autoencoder, followed by the full surrogate pipeline and a residual correction post-processor.

\section{Preliminary Study: Stationary Dataset}
\label{sec:stationary}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Problem Statement and Dataset}

The stationary test case is a convection-diffusion problem on the unit square $\Omega = [0,1]^2$:
\begin{equation}
    -D\,\Delta U + \bm{b} \cdot \nabla U = f \quad \text{in } \Omega,
    \qquad U = 0 \quad \text{on } \partial\Omega,
    \label{eq:convdiff}
\end{equation}
where $D > 0$ is the diffusivity, $\bm{b} = (b_x, b_y)$ is the convection velocity, and $f$ is a point-source intensity. The parameter vector $\vtheta = (D, b_x, b_y, f) \in \R^4$ fully specifies a solution instance, and the surrogate task is to learn the forward map $\vtheta \mapsto U \in \R^{N \times N}$.

The dataset is generated by uniformly sampling $\vtheta$ over the parameter domain and solving~\eqref{eq:convdiff} using P1 finite elements with SUPG/GLS stabilisation (FEniCS/DOLFINx). Solutions are interpolated onto a uniform $N \times N$ grid. For machine learning, each field $U$ is centred and rescaled to $[-1,1]$ using per-pixel statistics computed on the training set; the parameters $\vtheta$ are standardised using pre-computed mean and standard deviation. Representative examples are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:dataset}.

    \caption{Representative sample of the generated dataset.}
    \label{fig:dataset}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Model}

\subsubsection{Variational Autoencoder}

The first stage trains a variational autoencoder (VAE) \cite{kingma2013vae} to compress solution fields $U \in \R^{N \times N}$ into a low-dimensional latent code $z \in \R^{d_z}$ ($d_z = 32$).

\paragraph{Encoder.}
The encoder $q_\phi(z|U)$ applies four strided convolutional blocks (channels $1\!\to\!16\!\to\!32\!\to\!64\!\to\!128$, stride 2, LeakyReLU, total downsampling $16\times$), then produces $\mu$ and $\log\sigma^2$ via two parallel FC heads. The latent code is sampled via the reparameterisation trick at training time; at inference $z = \mu$.

\paragraph{Decoder.}
The decoder $p_\psi(U|z)$ projects $z$ to $128\cdot(N/32)^2$ features, upsamples through four transposed-convolutional blocks (channels $128\!\to\!64\!\to\!32\!\to\!16\!\to\!1$, BatchNorm + ReLU), and applies a final dense block mapping to the full $N^2$ grid, mitigating checkerboard artefacts.

\paragraph{Training objective.}
The model is trained by maximising the evidence lower bound (ELBO):
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{VAE}} = \E_{q(z|U)}\bigl[\|U - \hat{U}\|^2 + \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{grad}}(U, \hat{U})\bigr] + \beta\,\KL\bigl(q(z|U)\,\|\,\mathcal{N}(0,I)\bigr),
    \label{eq:elbo}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{grad}}$ is a spatially weighted gradient penalty focusing on sharp fronts. The KL uses a free-bits threshold ($\lambda_{\mathrm{fb}} = 0.1$ per dimension) to prevent posterior collapse, and $\beta$ is linearly annealed over the first 80 epochs.

\subsubsection{Indirect Decoder \texorpdfstring{$\vtheta \to z \to U$}{theta to z to U}}

The second stage decouples surrogate training from representation learning. Once the VAE is trained, its decoder $p_\psi$ is frozen and only a lightweight projection network is optimised.

\paragraph{Architecture.}
The projection network is a two-layer MLP:
\[
    \vtheta_{\mathrm{norm}} \;\xrightarrow{\mathrm{Lin}_{4\to128}}\; \mathrm{ReLU} \;\xrightarrow{\mathrm{Lin}_{128\to d_z}}\; \hat{z},
\]
where $\vtheta_{\mathrm{norm}} \in \R^4$ denotes the standardised parameter vector. The field estimate is then obtained by passing $\hat{z}$ through the frozen VAE decoder: $\hat{U} = p_\psi(\hat{z})$.

\paragraph{Training objective.}
The projection network is trained end-to-end (VAE decoder frozen) by minimising:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{dec}} = \|U - \hat{U}\|^2 + \frac{1}{2}\bigl(\|\partial_x\hat{U} - \partial_x U\|^2 + \|\partial_y\hat{U} - \partial_y U\|^2\bigr),
    \label{eq:dec_loss}
\end{equation}
combining pixel-level fidelity with a spatial gradient consistency term.

\subsection{Results}

The VAE is evaluated on 1\,000 held-out test samples (10\% of the full dataset of 10\,000 simulations) that were never seen during training. Reconstruction quality is measured by the relative $L^2$ error
\[
    \varepsilon_{\mathrm{rel}} = \frac{\|U - \hat{U}\|_2}{\|U\|_2} \times 100\%.
\]

\paragraph{Quantitative performance.}
Figure~\ref{fig:vae_hist} shows the distribution of $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{rel}}$ over the test set. The VAE achieves a \textbf{median error of 8.1\%} and a mean error of 11.5\% (std 9.8\%). The distribution is right-skewed: the bulk of samples is reconstructed with errors below 15\%, but a long tail extends to high values, corresponding to parameter configurations where the convection is strong (large $|\bm{b}|$) and produces sharp concentration gradients that are difficult to represent in the 32-dimensional latent code.

\subsection{Discussion}

The two-stage pipeline performs well on the stationary problem: the end-to-end surrogate reaches 9.9\% median error, close to the 8.1\% VAE floor. Three lessons from this study will directly inform the design of the transient surrogate.

\textbf{The autoencoder is the bottleneck, not the surrogate MLP.} The gap between the frozen-decoder variant (27.7\% median) and the end-to-end fine-tuned variant (9.9\%) demonstrates that the MLP regression $\vtheta \mapsto z$ is not the limiting factor: once the decoder is allowed to adapt, the surrogate closely tracks the AE reconstruction floor. This implies that any effort to improve the surrogate must first improve the latent representation, a principle that will guide every architectural decision in Section~\ref{sec:transient}.

\textbf{End-to-end fine-tuning is indispensable.} Keeping the decoder frozen forces the MLP to hit a precise target in a fixed latent geometry, which is a highly constrained regression problem. Unlocking the decoder and supervising on the output field instead of the latent code relaxes this constraint and dramatically reduces error. The same strategy (frozen pre-training followed by joint fine-tuning) will be reproduced in the transient pipeline.

\textbf{The temporal dimension requires a structural decomposition.} Extending the two-stage approach directly to transient fields is not straightforward: the output is now a tensor of shape $(N_t \times N \times N)$ per simulation, making direct regression or a single volumetric autoencoder computationally prohibitive. Rather than treating the full time series as a monolithic object, the approach adopted in Section~\ref{sec:transient} decomposes it along the temporal axis first. The Laplace transform projects each simulation onto a set of independent complex-valued 2D fields (one per frequency), each structurally analogous to the stationary fields studied here. This decomposition reduces the transient surrogate problem to a collection of stationary-like surrogate problems, to which the lessons above apply directly.

\section{Transient CH4 Concentration Fields}
\label{sec:transient}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Dataset and Temporal Representation}

\subsubsection{Dataset}

The transient experiments use a dataset of 8\,100 simulations of atmospheric methane (CH\textsubscript{4}) concentration fields. Each simulation yields a time series of 150 snapshots on a $200 \times 200$ spatial grid, forming a tensor of shape $(8100, 150, 200, 200)$. The physical parameter vector is $\vtheta = (k, A, C)$, where $k$ is the reaction rate, $A$ the rotation angle of the source plume, and $C$ the initial concentration. To accommodate the $\sim 17$ GB total dataset size, all data are loaded and processed using memory-mapped arrays, avoiding materialisation of the full tensor in RAM at any point.

The 8\,100 samples were obtained by data augmentation from 225 base simulations. In the original dataset, the source amplitude parameter was fixed at $A = 0$ for all simulations, leaving only $(k, C)$ as free parameters. To introduce directional variability and increase dataset size, each base simulation was replicated under 36 uniform rotations of the spatial field, with angles $\theta \in \{0^\circ, 10^\circ, \ldots, 350^\circ\}$, giving $225 \times 36 = 8\,100$ samples. The rotation angle $\theta$ is stored as the parameter $A$ in the dataset, so the effective parameter space explored is $(k, \theta, C)$. Rotations were applied frame-by-frame using bilinear interpolation, with border replication to avoid introducing artificial zero-padding artefacts.

Figure~\ref{fig:ch4_dataset} shows a representative example of the transient field evolution. The dataset captures a reactive dispersion process in which methane undergoes chemical reaction from a point source, producing sharp concentration gradients and fast early-time transients. This \emph{explosion-like} onset is a key challenge: surrogates that fail to resolve it accumulate error that propagates through the subsequent diffusion phase.

\subsubsection{Laplace Transform}

Rather than operating in physical time, the surrogate works in the Laplace frequency domain. The Laplace transform of a time-dependent field is approximated via a discretised Bromwich contour integral, computed efficiently using the discrete Fourier transform with exponential damping:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{U}(s_k) = \int_0^T U(t)\,e^{-s_k t}\,\mathrm{d}t
    \approx \mathrm{DFT}\bigl[U(t) \cdot w_t \cdot e^{-\gamma t}\bigr],
    \qquad s_k = \gamma + i\omega_k,
    \label{eq:laplace}
\end{equation}
where $w_t$ are trapezoidal integration weights and $\gamma \geq 0$ is a damping parameter that improves numerical stability. The spatial field $U$ is transformed at each frequency $s_k$ into a complex-valued field $\hat{U}(s_k) \in \C^{N \times N}$. For real-valued physical fields, Hermitian symmetry provides $\hat{U}(s_{N_t-k}) = \overline{\hat{U}(s_k)}$, so only $N_{\mathrm{half}} = N_t/2 + 1$ frequencies need be stored. In practice, each frequency $s_k$ is represented as the pair of real fields $(\Re(\hat{U}_k), \Im(\hat{U}_k)) \in \R^{2 \times N \times N}$.

The surrogate models only the first $k_{\max}$ frequencies (specifically, $k_{\max} = 20$), which typically capture the essential dynamics while reducing computational cost. Higher frequencies ($k > k_{\max}$) are set to zero in the normalised representation, corresponding to the mean field after denormalisation. Inversion to physical time is performed by discrete Fourier inversion with exponential compensation on the full reconstructed spectrum.

\subsection{Latent Space Representation}

\subsubsection{Tucker POD Surrogate}

\paragraph{Algorithm.}
The Tucker POD method, introduced by Ammar \emph{et al.}~\cite{ammar2024hybrid}, computes a greedy rank-$(1,1,1)$ Tucker decomposition of the full training tensor by sequentially extracting separable modes. Given the three-dimensional tensor $\bm{H} \in \R^{n_r \times n_s \times N_t}$, the decomposition seeks an approximation:
\begin{equation}
    H_{r,s,t} \approx \sum_{j=1}^{n_f} \alpha_j\, F_{r,j}\, G_{s,j}\, P_{t,j},
    \label{eq:tucker}
\end{equation}
where $\bm{F}$, $\bm{P}$, $\bm{G}$ collect spatial, temporal, and simulation-dependent modes respectively, and $\bm{\alpha}$ the singular values. Each mode is extracted from the current residual by a rank-1 ALS problem; starting from random vectors $R, S, T$, the updates are:
\begin{equation}
    R \leftarrow \frac{\bm{R}^{(j)}_{(1)}\,(T \otimes S)}{\|S\|^2\,\|T\|^2},
    \qquad
    S \leftarrow \frac{\bm{R}^{(j)}_{(2)}\,(R \otimes T)}{\|R\|^2\,\|T\|^2},
    \qquad
    T \leftarrow \frac{\bm{R}^{(j)}_{(3)}\,(S \otimes R)}{\|R\|^2\,\|S\|^2},
    \label{eq:als}
\end{equation}
where $\bm{R}^{(j)}_{(k)}$ is the mode-$k$ unfolding. After convergence, the normalised vectors are stored, the residual is deflated, and the process repeats until the relative residual $\|\bm{R}^{(j)}\|_F / \|\bm{H}\|_F$ falls below tolerance or $n_f$ modes are extracted.

\paragraph{Application to the CH4 dataset.}
The decomposition is applied to the full training set of $n_s = 8\,100$ simulations. Because the algorithm requires the entire tensor to reside in memory simultaneously, the spatial grid is subsampled with stride $\delta = 5$, reducing each $200 \times 200$ frame to a $40 \times 40$ grid ($n_r = 1\,600$ nodes). Up to $n_f = 300$ modes are extracted; the convergence of the relative residual as a function of mode index is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:svd}(a).

\paragraph{Surrogate training.}
The spatial factors $\bm{F}$, temporal factors $\bm{P}$, and singular values $\bm{\alpha}$ are fixed after the Tucker decomposition and stored in the checkpoint. The simulation-dependent coefficient vector $\bm{g} \in \R^{n_f}$ (the $s$-th row of $\bm{G}$) encodes the identity of each simulation in a compact latent space. A four-layer MLP is trained to predict these coefficients from the physical parameters: $\vtheta_{\mathrm{norm}} \mapsto \hat{\bm{g}} \in \R^{n_f}$. At inference time, the full spatiotemporal field on the subsampled grid is reconstructed as
\[
    \hat{H}_{r,t} = \sum_{j=1}^{n_f} \alpha_j\, F_{r,j}\, \hat{g}_j\, P_{t,j}.
\]

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The test-set error reported in Figure~\ref{fig:svd}(b) conflates two independent sources of error. First, the Tucker decomposition itself introduces a \emph{representation error}: the best rank-$n_f$ approximation of $\bm{H}$ is not exact, and the relative residual after convergence is approximately 10\%. This is a floor that no surrogate, however accurate, can overcome. Second, the MLP must learn a non-linear map $\vtheta \mapsto \bm{g}$; any error in predicting the coefficient vector propagates through the reconstruction formula and adds to the representation error. The observed test-set median error of around 30--40\% thus reflects the cumulation of these two contributions. Importantly, this error is evaluated on the downsampled $40\times40$ grid, not on the original $200\times200$ resolution; the Laplace-based surrogates in the following sections are evaluated at $128\times128$, so these figures are \emph{not directly comparable}.

A more fundamental constraint stems from the batch nature of the algorithm: equations~\eqref{eq:als} require the \emph{entire} training tensor $\bm{H}$ to reside in GPU memory simultaneously. With $n_r = 1\,600$, $n_s = 8\,100$, and $N_t = 150$ in single precision, this amounts to approximately 7.8 GB. Increasing the spatial resolution directly scales this requirement: restoring the full $200 \times 200$ grid would require $25\times$ more memory ($\sim 193$ GB), which is infeasible. Consequently, the Tucker POD approach is confined to the downsampled $40 \times 40$ representation, which discards spatial details at scales finer than five grid cells. This resolution bottleneck, combined with the non-negligible representation error, motivates the exploration of surrogate methods that operate in a transformed space where the dimensionality is more manageable.

\subsubsection{SVD Surrogate in the Laplace Domain}

An alternative linear approach applies truncated SVD independently to each Laplace frequency. For frequency $k$, the real and imaginary parts of $\hat{U}_k$ across all training samples are arranged into a matrix, and the leading $k_{\mathrm{svd}}$ right singular vectors $\bm{V}^{(k,r)}$ and $\bm{V}^{(k,i)}$ are extracted:
\[
    \Re(\hat{U}_k) \approx \bm{c}^{(k,r)} \bigl(\bm{V}^{(k,r)}\bigr)^\top, \qquad
    \Im(\hat{U}_k) \approx \bm{c}^{(k,i)} \bigl(\bm{V}^{(k,i)}\bigr)^\top.
\]
The spatial basis $\bm{V}$ is fixed during training. For each frequency $k$, a dedicated three-layer MLP learns to predict the normalised coefficients $\bm{c}^{(k,r)}$ and $\bm{c}^{(k,i)}$ from the physical parameters. Field reconstruction follows by projecting the predicted coefficients onto the fixed bases; the full time series is then recovered by inverting the Laplace transform as in~\eqref{eq:laplace}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.70\textwidth]{LaplaceSVDModel_svd_error_ksvd100.png}
    \caption{SVD reconstruction error vs.\ $k_{\mathrm{svd}}$ for the per-frequency SVD approach.}
    \label{fig:lapsvd}
\end{figure}

While the per-frequency SVD factorisation operates on the full $200 \times 200$ spatial resolution (circumventing the memory bottleneck of Tucker POD), it faces its own irreducible limitation: the linear basis truncation error is already substantial at the lowest frequencies (5--10\%) and grows monotonically with frequency. Consequently, the MLP surrogates are trained on noisy targets that are only approximate representations of the true Laplace fields. The combination of fixed linear bases with inherent reconstruction loss and complex non-linear prediction tasks yields poor generalisation on the test set, motivating the transition to a learned, non-linear autoencoder.

\subsubsection{Autoencoder-Based Latent Space}

Both SVD approaches above constrain the surrogate to lie in a fixed linear subspace extracted from the training data. When Laplace fields exhibit non-linear variation across the parameter space, this linearity becomes restrictive. To overcome this limitation, a learned autoencoder is trained to extract a non-linear latent representation that is shared across all frequencies. The encoder and decoder are conditioned on the frequency index $k$, allowing a single pair of networks to process fields at any frequency while adapting their behaviour through frequency-specific control signals.

\paragraph{Sinusoidal frequency encoding.}
The frequency ratio $f = k/N_{\mathrm{half}} \in [0,1]$ is embedded via sinusoidal basis functions inspired by NeRF positional encoding \cite{mildenhall2020nerf}:
\begin{equation}
    \bm{e}(f) = \mathrm{MLP}\Bigl(\bigl[\sin(2^i\pi f),\; \cos(2^i\pi f)\bigr]_{i=0}^{L-1}\Bigr), \qquad L = 8.
    \label{eq:posenc}
\end{equation}
A plain scalar $f$ would conflate nearby frequencies and fail to capture the sharp transition in field structure between low and high frequencies. The multi-scale sinusoidal basis explicitly represents $f$ at $L$ different resolutions, providing the network with a rich, non-saturating signal that allows fine discrimination across the full frequency range.

\paragraph{FiLM conditioning.}
To share encoder/decoder weights across all frequencies while adapting to each one, the FiLM mechanism \cite{perez2018film} is employed. At each convolutional layer $\ell$, the frequency embedding $\bm{e}(f)$ is projected to a channel-wise gain $\gamma_\ell \in \R^{C_\ell}$ and bias $\beta_\ell \in \R^{C_\ell}$, modulating the activations element-wise:
\begin{equation}
    \bm{x}_\ell \;\leftarrow\; \bm{x}_\ell \odot \bigl(1 + \gamma_\ell(\bm{e}(f))\bigr) \;+\; \beta_\ell(\bm{e}(f)).
    \label{eq:film}
\end{equation}
The residual formulation $(1 + \gamma_\ell)$ initialises the modulation close to the identity, which stabilises early training. All frequencies share the same convolutional weights, and the FiLM parameters act as a lightweight per-frequency adapter at each layer, enabling a single autoencoder to process the full spectrum without per-frequency model copies.

\paragraph{Architecture and training.}
The autoencoder encoder takes a two-channel input $(\Re(\hat{U}_k), \Im(\hat{U}_k)) \in \R^{2 \times N \times N}$ representing the real and imaginary parts of a Laplace field at frequency $k$, and progressively downsamples it through strided convolutions to produce a latent code $z \in \R^{d_z}$. Each convolutional block is followed by FiLM modulation using the frequency embedding $\bm{e}(f)$. The decoder reverses this process, starting from $z$ and upsampling through transposed convolutions, also FiLM-conditioned at each layer. The decoder outputs two separate branches, one predicting $\Re(\hat{U}_k)$ and the other $\Im(\hat{U}_k)$.

Training operates on the Cartesian product of all (simulation, frequency) pairs in the dataset, with the objective:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{AE}} = \|\hat{U} - U\|^2 + \beta\,\|\hat{U}\|^2,
    \label{eq:aeloss}
\end{equation}
where the first term is the reconstruction MSE and the second is an $L^2$ regularisation penalty on the output. To ensure diverse frequency coverage within each mini-batch, (simulation, frequency) pairs are reshuffled at the start of each epoch. Training and validation $L^2$ relative errors (Figure~\ref{fig:ae_training}) converge in lockstep to approximately 10.50\% over 100 epochs, with no measurable overfitting.

\textbf{Choice of $k_{\max}$.}
The monotone increase in AE error justifies limiting surrogate training to $k_{\max} = 20$ out of $N_{\text{half}} = 76$ frequencies. Beyond $k=20$, reconstruction quality degrades sharply and any surrogate would inherit substantial baseline errors. Unmodelled frequencies are replaced by the per-pixel training mean upon inversion, avoiding training on irreducibly noisy targets.

\paragraph{Limitations of the frequency-conditioned autoencoder.}
Two structural limitations are worth noting. First, FiLM conditioning means there is no single shared latent geometry: the code for $k=0$ lives in a different effective space than the code for $k=20$, preventing cross-frequency regularisation and making each per-frequency surrogate navigate its own latent manifold. Second, the 9.94\% temporal reconstruction floor sets the minimum error achievable by any downstream surrogate. In practice, this floor is mitigated by end-to-end fine-tuning (Section~\ref{sec:finetune}), which allows the decoder to adapt and recover artefacts introduced by the fixed AE.

\subsection{Surrogate and Fine-Tuning}
\label{sec:finetune}

\subsubsection{Per-Frequency Surrogate \texorpdfstring{$\vtheta \to z$}{theta to z}}

Once the frequency-conditioned autoencoder has been trained, its decoder is frozen, and independent surrogates are trained for each frequency. The surrogate operates only on the first $k_{\max}$ frequencies (with $k_{\max} = 20$); higher frequencies are not explicitly predicted. For frequencies $k = 0, 1, \ldots, k_{\max}$, a four-layer MLP with layer normalisation learns the mapping from normalised physical parameters to the latent code: $\vtheta_{\mathrm{norm}} \mapsto z_k \in \R^{d_z}$. The shared decoder is kept frozen to preserve the learned latent structure.

At inference time, each predicted code $z_k$ is decoded using the frozen decoder, which is itself conditioned on the frequency ratio $f_k$ via FiLM, yielding predictions for frequencies $k \leq k_{\max}$. Frequencies $k > k_{\max}$ are left at zero in the normalised space, which corresponds to the mean value after denormalisation. The complete Laplace spectrum is then obtained by Hermitian symmetry (the upper frequencies are conjugates of the lower ones), and the time-domain field is recovered by Laplace inversion as in~\eqref{eq:laplace}. \paragraph{Training dynamics.}
Surrogates are trained sequentially one frequency at a time, producing the sawtooth pattern in Figures~\ref{fig:surrogate_training_loss}--\ref{fig:surrogate_training_l2rel}. The upward drift in the loss envelope reflects two compounding effects: regression difficulty increases with frequency (more oscillatory, more non-linear fields), and the AE reconstruction floor itself grows from $\sim$7\% at $k=0$ to $\sim$14\% at $k=20$.

\subsubsection{End-to-End Fine-Tuning}

To further improve accuracy, the frozen decoder is unfrozen and the entire model—both the per-frequency MLPs and the shared decoder—is fine-tuned jointly end-to-end on the training set. A key feature of this stage is that the loss is computed directly on the physical-time field $U(t)$, not on the Laplace spectra: since the inverse Laplace transform is differentiable (as discussed in Section~\ref{sec:transient}), the full chain $\vtheta \to z_k \to \hat{U}_k \to U(t)$ can be optimised end-to-end. This choice is deliberate: supervising on $U(t)$ naturally prioritises the frequencies that matter most for prediction quality, rather than treating each frequency uniformly. Differential learning rates are employed: $\eta_{\mathrm{surr}} = 5 \times 10^{-5}$ for the per-frequency MLPs and $\eta_{\mathrm{dec}} = 10^{-5}$ for the decoder. Training and validation curves are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:finetune_training}: both decrease steadily from $\sim$16\% to 10.50\% over 50 epochs. Training has not fully converged, but joint fine-tuning is significantly slower than frozen-decoder training (each forward pass requires a full Laplace inversion), which practically limits the tractable $k_{\max}$.

\paragraph{Limitations: Laplace truncation and Gibbs effects.}
Despite the substantial gains from fine-tuning, a fundamental limitation arises from the truncation of the Laplace spectrum. The surrogate predicts only the first $k_{\max} = 20$ frequencies out of the available $N_{\mathrm{half}} = 51$ frequencies (for $N_t = 150$ snapshots). Higher frequencies are set to zero during inversion, effectively replacing the truncated spectrum with the mean field. This spectral truncation introduces \emph{Gibbs phenomenon}---a well-known oscillatory artefact that appears near sharp transitions in fields reconstructed from truncated orthogonal expansions. The high-frequency details necessary to capture sharp concentration gradients or rapid transients are lost, creating residual ripples that the surrogate cannot overcome. This truncation error manifests as a prediction floor around 6--8\% median error, below which further improvements require either: (i) training on more frequencies, (ii) using a larger $k_{\max}$, or (iii) applying non-spectral surrogate approaches. The residual corrector, described next, partially alleviates this limitation by learning to suppress Gibbs oscillations frame by frame.



\subsection{Post-Processing and Results}

\subsubsection{Residual Correction}

A UNet \cite{ronneberger2015unet} residual corrector is applied post hoc, frame by frame, to the surrogate output. A UNet is an encoder--decoder convolutional network in which the encoder progressively downsamples the spatial resolution through successive convolution and pooling operations, while the decoder restores the original resolution through transposed convolutions. At each resolution level, the decoder receives, via a skip connection, the feature maps produced by the encoder at the corresponding level; these are concatenated channel-wise before the next convolution. This architecture allows the network to combine coarse, context-rich features from the bottleneck with fine spatial detail preserved by the skip connections. A standard autoencoder forces all information through the bottleneck, discarding spatial detail that cannot be encoded in the latent code; the skip connections of the UNet bypass this bottleneck, providing the decoder with direct access to high-resolution encoder features and enabling precise spatial localisation in the output.

Given the surrogate prediction $U_{\mathrm{pred}}(t) \in \R^{N \times N}$, the residual correction operates as follows. The predicted field is first normalised per-frame using its own spatial statistics (mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$), then passed through a UNet that predicts a residual correction in the normalised space. The residual is rescaled back to physical space and added to the original prediction:
\begin{equation}
    U_{\mathrm{corr}} = U_{\mathrm{pred}} + \sigma(U_{\mathrm{pred}}) \cdot r\!\left(\frac{U_{\mathrm{pred}} - \mu(U_{\mathrm{pred}})}{\sigma(U_{\mathrm{pred}})}\right),
    \label{eq:correction}
\end{equation}
Here $r(\cdot)$ is a three-level UNet with skip connections, employing $c = 16$ base channels and approximately 481k parameters. A crucial design choice is that the final output layer of the UNet is zero-initialised, ensuring that $r \equiv 0$ at the start of training. This initialisation strategy causes the corrected field to equal the surrogate prediction initially, allowing the network to learn only the correction needed rather than the full field.

\subsubsection{Training the Corrector}

A key practical consideration in training the residual corrector is computational efficiency. Rather than calling the frozen surrogate model repeatedly during training, all $(U_{\mathrm{pred}}, U_{\mathrm{true}})$ pairs are pre-computed and stored on disk as memory-mapped arrays before the corrector training begins. This pre-computation step ensures that no forward pass through the surrogate is needed during the training loop, significantly accelerating convergence.

The corrector loss combines reconstruction accuracy with spatial smoothness. Denoting $r_{\mathrm{pred}} = U_{\mathrm{corr}} - U_{\mathrm{pred}}$ as the predicted residual and $r_{\mathrm{true}} = U_{\mathrm{true}} - U_{\mathrm{pred}}$ as the target residual, the loss function is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{corr}} = \left\|\frac{r_{\mathrm{pred}} - r_{\mathrm{true}}}{\sigma_r}\right\|^2 + \lambda_{\nabla}\left\|\frac{\nabla r_{\mathrm{pred}} - \nabla r_{\mathrm{true}}}{\sigma_r}\right\|^2,
    \label{eq:corrloss}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_r = \sigma(r_{\mathrm{true}})$ is the standard deviation of the target residual field, and $\lambda_{\nabla} = 10$ is the gradient weighting parameter. Both the residual MSE and the spatial gradient MSE are normalised by $\sigma_r$, which accounts for the magnitude of the true correction and prevents the loss from being dominated by frames requiring large corrections.

The training and validation $L^2$ relative errors over 100 epochs are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:correction_training}: both decrease from $\sim$9\% to 4.46\%, with a small train--val gap appearing from epoch 60 onward due to the sparse temporal sampling ($k_t = 20$ frames per simulation) used during training.

\paragraph{Complete inference pipeline.}
The three refinement stages compose a single end-to-end inference pipeline summarised in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}. Given a parameter vector $\vtheta \in \mathbb{R}^3$, the model first normalises the input and maps it through 21 independent latent projections --- one per modelled Laplace frequency --- to produce latent codes $z_0, \ldots, z_{20}$. A single batched call to the shared, FiLM-conditioned decoder then reconstructs the normalised Laplace fields for all frequencies simultaneously. After denormalisation, the full spectrum is completed by Hermitian symmetry and inverted to the time domain via the discrete Laplace transform. The resulting surrogate field $U_{\mathrm{surr}}(t)$ is finally passed frame by frame through the residual UNet corrector, which suppresses Gibbs oscillations and sharpens spatial gradients, producing the corrected output $\hat{U}_{\mathrm{corr}}(t)$.

\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

This work presents a hierarchy of neural surrogate architectures for PDE emulation centred on learned latent representations. On the stationary problem, a VAE two-stage approach decouples representation learning from parameter regression. On the transient problem, Tucker POD and per-frequency SVD provide linear baselines, while a frequency-conditioned autoencoder with sinusoidal encoding and FiLM modulation learns a non-linear latent space shared across all Laplace frequencies. Per-frequency MLPs map the physical parameters into this latent space; end-to-end fine-tuning then aligns the surrogate with the decoder. A UNet residual corrector applied post hoc further reduces prediction error at marginal cost.

Future directions include incorporating physics-informed constraints into the latent space, extending the framework to varying source geometries, and applying the residual corrector to other reduced-order surrogate families.
