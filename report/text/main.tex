\begin{abstract}
We investigate neural surrogate models for PDE emulation, centred on learning a compact latent representation of solution fields before regressing physical parameters into that space. On a stationary 2-D convection-diffusion problem, a variational autoencoder (VAE) combined with end-to-end fine-tuning achieves a median relative $L^2$ error of 9.9\%, close to the 8.1\% reconstruction floor. On a transient atmospheric methane (CH\textsubscript{4}) dispersion dataset ($8\,100$ simulations, $150$ time steps, $200\!\times\!200$ spatial grid), the high output dimensionality is managed by working in the Laplace frequency domain, which decomposes each simulation into independent 2-D fields amenable to a shared convolutional autoencoder. Linear baselines (Tucker POD, per-frequency SVD) are evaluated and their representational limitations identified. A three-stage hierarchy is then proposed: (i) per-frequency MLP surrogates mapping physical parameters to latent codes (median error 18.5\%); (ii) end-to-end fine-tuning on the physical-time loss, reducing median error to 9.3\%; and (iii) a lightweight UNet residual corrector suppressing Gibbs oscillations, reaching \textbf{4.46\%} median error at a marginal overhead of 481k parameters. A consistent finding is that the autoencoder is the performance bottleneck and that end-to-end fine-tuning is indispensable.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

The numerical simulation of physical systems governed by partial differential equations (PDEs) is central to a wide range of scientific and engineering disciplines, from atmospheric transport modelling and chemical process engineering to structural mechanics and geophysics. High-fidelity solvers—based on finite elements, finite volumes, or spectral methods—can faithfully capture complex multi-scale phenomena, but at a computational cost that renders repeated evaluations impractical. Real-time monitoring, Bayesian inverse problems, optimal experimental design, and digital twin frameworks all demand thousands to millions of forward evaluations; each solver query may take minutes to hours, making exhaustive parameter exploration computationally intractable \cite{ammar2024hybrid,quarteroni2025}.

This tension between fidelity and cost motivates \emph{surrogate modelling}: replacing the solver by a cheap, approximate emulator trained on a dataset of precomputed solutions. Classical methods such as Gaussian processes or polynomial chaos expansions offer principled uncertainty quantification but scale poorly with the dimension of both the input parameter space and the spatial field output. Deep learning surrogates can in principle learn highly non-linear parameter-to-field maps at full resolution, but naive regression onto $N_t \times N^2$ output values per simulation remains intractable for realistic grids and time horizons \cite{karniadakis2021,quarteroni2025}.

The central difficulty is therefore one of \emph{representation}: the surrogate must first find a compact encoding of the solution manifold before it can learn to navigate it. A natural strategy is to decouple these two problems—learn a latent representation of the solution fields, then train a lightweight map from physical parameters into this latent space. This two-stage paradigm forms the backbone of every architecture explored in this work.

\textbf{Application context.}
The motivating application is the emulation of atmospheric methane (CH\textsubscript{4}) dispersion models for real-time pollutant monitoring and inverse source identification \cite{fan2024,guo2022}. Each simulation produces a time series of 150 snapshots on a $200 \times 200$ grid; the parameter space spans complex non-linear variations including sharp early-time transients that standard low-rank methods struggle to capture.

\textbf{Approach and contributions.}
We investigate a hierarchy of latent-space strategies of increasing expressiveness. Starting with a stationary 2-D convection-diffusion problem as a controlled testbed, we validate a variational autoencoder (VAE) two-stage pipeline in which a frozen decoder is subsequently adapted by end-to-end fine-tuning. The key lesson—that the autoencoder is the bottleneck, and that joint fine-tuning is indispensable—directly informs every design decision on the transient problem.

On the transient dataset, the high dimensionality of spatiotemporal fields is managed by working in the Laplace frequency domain: the numerical Bromwich integral transforms each simulation into $N_{\mathrm{half}}$ independent complex-valued 2-D fields, each structurally analogous to the stationary case. We evaluate two linear baselines (Tucker POD and per-frequency SVD) before introducing a frequency-conditioned autoencoder with sinusoidal positional encoding and FiLM modulation that learns a non-linear latent space shared across all Laplace frequencies. Per-frequency MLP surrogates map physical parameters into this latent space; end-to-end fine-tuning closes the gap between the surrogate and the autoencoder floor. A final lightweight UNet residual corrector, trained offline on precomputed prediction pairs, suppresses the Gibbs oscillations introduced by spectral truncation and reduces median error to 4.46\%—a factor-of-four improvement over the unfinetuned baseline.

\textbf{Outline.}
Section~\ref{sec:stationary} presents the stationary testbed and the lessons drawn for the transient problem. Section~\ref{sec:transient} covers the CH\textsubscript{4} dataset, the Laplace representation, the surrogate pipeline, and the quantitative benchmark.

\section{Preliminary Study: Stationary Dataset}
\label{sec:stationary}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Problem Statement and Dataset}

The stationary test case is a convection-diffusion problem on the unit square $\Omega = [0,1]^2$:
\begin{equation}
    -D\,\Delta U + \bm{b} \cdot \nabla U = f \quad \text{in } \Omega,
    \qquad U = 0 \quad \text{on } \partial\Omega,
    \label{eq:convdiff}
\end{equation}
where $D > 0$ is the diffusivity, $\bm{b} = (b_x, b_y)$ is the convection velocity, and $f$ is a point-source intensity. The parameter vector $\vtheta = (D, b_x, b_y, f) \in \R^4$ fully specifies a solution instance, and the surrogate task is to learn the forward map $\vtheta \mapsto U \in \R^{N \times N}$.

The dataset is generated by uniformly sampling $\vtheta$ over the parameter domain and solving~\eqref{eq:convdiff} using P1 finite elements with SUPG/GLS stabilisation (FEniCS/DOLFINx). Solutions are interpolated onto a uniform $N \times N$ grid. For machine learning, each field $U$ is centred and rescaled to $[-1,1]$ using per-pixel statistics computed on the training set; the parameters $\vtheta$ are standardised using pre-computed mean and standard deviation. Representative examples are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:dataset}.

    \centering

    \caption{Representative sample of the generated dataset.}
    \label{fig:dataset}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Model}

\subsubsection{Variational Autoencoder}

The first stage trains a variational autoencoder (VAE) \cite{kingma2013vae} to compress solution fields $U \in \R^{N \times N}$ into a low-dimensional latent code $z \in \R^{d_z}$ ($d_z = 32$).

\paragraph{Encoder.}
The encoder $q_\phi(z|U)$ applies four strided convolutional blocks (channels $1\!\to\!16\!\to\!32\!\to\!64\!\to\!128$, stride 2, LeakyReLU, total downsampling $16\times$), then produces $\mu$ and $\log\sigma^2$ via two parallel FC heads. The latent code is sampled via the reparameterisation trick at training time; at inference $z = \mu$.

\paragraph{Decoder.}
The decoder $p_\psi(U|z)$ projects $z$ to $128\cdot(N/32)^2$ features, upsamples through four transposed-convolutional blocks (channels $128\!\to\!64\!\to\!32\!\to\!16\!\to\!1$, BatchNorm + ReLU), and applies a final dense block mapping to the full $N^2$ grid, mitigating checkerboard artefacts.

\paragraph{Training objective.}
The model is trained by maximising the evidence lower bound (ELBO):
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{VAE}} = \E_{q(z|U)}\bigl[\|U - \hat{U}\|^2 + \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{grad}}(U, \hat{U})\bigr] + \beta\,\KL\bigl(q(z|U)\,\|\,\mathcal{N}(0,I)\bigr),
    \label{eq:elbo}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{grad}}$ is a spatially weighted gradient penalty focusing on sharp fronts. The KL uses a free-bits threshold ($\lambda_{\mathrm{fb}} = 0.1$ per dimension) to prevent posterior collapse, and $\beta$ is linearly annealed over the first 80 epochs.

\subsubsection{Indirect Decoder \texorpdfstring{$\vtheta \to z \to U$}{theta to z to U}}

The second stage decouples surrogate training from representation learning. Once the VAE is trained, its decoder $p_\psi$ is frozen and only a lightweight projection network is optimised.

\paragraph{Architecture.}
The projection network is a two-layer MLP:
\[
    \vtheta_{\mathrm{norm}} \;\xrightarrow{\mathrm{Lin}_{4\to128}}\; \mathrm{ReLU} \;\xrightarrow{\mathrm{Lin}_{128\to d_z}}\; \hat{z},
\]
where $\vtheta_{\mathrm{norm}} \in \R^4$ denotes the standardised parameter vector. The field estimate is then obtained by passing $\hat{z}$ through the frozen VAE decoder: $\hat{U} = p_\psi(\hat{z})$.

\paragraph{Training objective.}
The projection network is trained end-to-end (VAE decoder frozen) by minimising:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{dec}} = \|U - \hat{U}\|^2 + \frac{1}{2}\bigl(\|\partial_x\hat{U} - \partial_x U\|^2 + \|\partial_y\hat{U} - \partial_y U\|^2\bigr),
    \label{eq:dec_loss}
\end{equation}
combining pixel-level fidelity with a spatial gradient consistency term.

\subsection{Results}

The VAE is evaluated on 1\,000 held-out test samples (10\% of the full dataset of 10\,000 simulations) that were never seen during training. Reconstruction quality is measured by the relative $L^2$ error
\[
    \varepsilon_{\mathrm{rel}} = \frac{\|U - \hat{U}\|_2}{\|U\|_2} \times 100\%.
\]

\paragraph{Quantitative performance.}
Figure~\ref{fig:vae_hist} shows the distribution of $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{rel}}$ over the test set. The VAE achieves a \textbf{median error of 8.1\%} and a mean error of 11.5\% (std 9.8\%). The distribution is right-skewed: the bulk of samples is reconstructed with errors below 15\%, but a long tail extends to high values, corresponding to parameter configurations where the convection is strong (large $|\bm{b}|$) and produces sharp concentration gradients; these configurations are under-represented in the training set and are therefore harder to generalise to.

\subsection{Discussion}

The two-stage pipeline performs well on the stationary problem: the end-to-end surrogate reaches 9.9\% median error, close to the 8.1\% VAE floor. Two lessons will directly inform the transient surrogate. First, the autoencoder is the bottleneck: once the decoder is freed to adapt, the surrogate closely tracks the reconstruction floor, confirming that improving the latent representation is the highest-leverage action. Second, end-to-end fine-tuning is indispensable: frozen-decoder training yields errors two to three times higher, regardless of MLP capacity.

\textbf{Bridging to the transient problem.} Extending the two-stage approach directly to transient fields is not straightforward: the output is now a tensor of shape $(N_t \times N \times N)$ per simulation, making direct regression computationally prohibitive. The approach adopted in Section~\ref{sec:transient} decomposes the time series along the temporal axis via the Laplace transform, projecting each simulation onto a set of independent complex-valued 2D fields—each structurally analogous to the stationary fields studied here. This reduces the transient surrogate to a collection of stationary-like problems, to which the lessons above apply directly.

\section{Transient CH4 Concentration Fields}
\label{sec:transient}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Dataset and Temporal Representation}

\subsubsection{Dataset}

The transient experiments use a dataset of 8\,100 simulations of atmospheric methane (CH\textsubscript{4}) concentration fields. Each simulation yields a time series of 150 snapshots on a $200 \times 200$ spatial grid, forming a tensor of shape $(8100, 150, 200, 200)$. The physical parameter vector is $\vtheta = (k, A, C)$, where $k$ is the reaction rate, $A$ the rotation angle of the source plume, and $C$ the initial concentration. To accommodate the $\sim 17$ GB total dataset size, all data are loaded and processed using memory-mapped arrays, avoiding materialisation of the full tensor in RAM at any point.

The 8\,100 samples were obtained by data augmentation from 225 base simulations drawn from a regular grid over $(k, C)$. In the original dataset, the source amplitude parameter was fixed at $A = 0$ for all simulations, leaving only $(k, C)$ as free parameters. To introduce directional variability and increase dataset size, each base simulation was replicated under 36 uniform rotations of the spatial field, with angles $\theta \in \{0^\circ, 10^\circ, \ldots, 350^\circ\}$, giving $225 \times 36 = 8\,100$ samples. The rotation angle $\theta$ is stored as the parameter $A$ in the dataset, so the effective parameter space explored is $(k, A, C)$. Rotations were applied frame-by-frame using bilinear interpolation, with border replication to avoid introducing artificial zero-padding artefacts.

Figure~\ref{fig:ch4_dataset} shows a representative example of the transient field evolution. The dataset captures a reactive dispersion process in which methane undergoes chemical reaction from a point source, producing sharp concentration gradients and fast early-time transients. This \emph{explosion-like} onset is a key challenge: surrogates that fail to resolve it accumulate error that propagates through the subsequent diffusion phase.

\subsubsection{Laplace Transform}

Rather than operating in physical time, the surrogate works in the Laplace frequency domain. The Laplace transform of a time-dependent field is approximated via a discretised Bromwich contour integral, computed efficiently using the discrete Fourier transform with exponential damping:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{U}(s_k) = \int_0^T U(t)\,e^{-s_k t}\,\mathrm{d}t
    \;\approx\; \Delta t \sum_{n=0}^{N_t-1} w_n\, U_n\, e^{-\gamma t_n} e^{-i\omega_k t_n},
    \qquad s_k = \gamma + i\omega_k,
    \label{eq:laplace}
\end{equation}
where $U_n = U(t_n)$, $t_n = n\Delta t$, $\omega_k = 2\pi k/(N_t \Delta t)$, $w_n$ are trapezoidal integration weights, and $\gamma \geq 0$ is a damping parameter. The spatial field $U$ is transformed at each frequency $s_k$ into a complex-valued field $\hat{U}(s_k) \in \C^{N \times N}$. For real-valued physical fields, Hermitian symmetry provides $\hat{U}(s_{N_t-k}) = \overline{\hat{U}(s_k)}$, so only $N_{\mathrm{half}} = N_t/2 + 1$ frequencies need be stored. In practice, each frequency $s_k$ is represented as the pair of real fields $(\Re(\hat{U}_k), \Im(\hat{U}_k)) \in \R^{2 \times N \times N}$.

\paragraph{Bromwich contour and discretisation.}\label{par:differentiability}
The Bromwich inversion formula integrates $\hat{U}(s)\,e^{st}$ along a vertical contour $\{s = \gamma + i\omega\}$ located to the right of all singularities of $\hat{U}$ \cite{abate1992fourier}. For well-posed PDEs, all poles lie in the left half-plane, so any $\gamma \geq 0$ is valid. The weight $e^{-\gamma t}$ acts as a regularising window suppressing aliasing; the damping is compensated during inversion by multiplying by $e^{\gamma t}$. Since both the forward and inverse transforms are linear operations with fixed weights, they are fully differentiable, allowing a loss on $U(t)$ to be backpropagated through the Laplace inversion all the way to the predicted coefficients $\hat{U}_k$.

\paragraph{Spectral truncation.}
The Laplace spectrum decays rapidly with frequency: Figure~\ref{fig:spectral_energy} shows the mean spectral energy $\mathbb{E}_s\bigl[\sum_{x}\lvert\hat{U}_k(x)\rvert^2\bigr]$ per frequency $k$, decreasing by several orders of magnitude between $k=0$ and $k=N_{\mathrm{half}}$. The bulk of the signal energy is concentrated in the first $\sim$20 frequencies. Modelling all $N_{\mathrm{half}}$ frequencies would be computationally prohibitive and the high-frequency fields contribute little to the physical reconstruction. The surrogate therefore models only the first $k_{\max}$ frequencies; higher frequencies ($k > k_{\max}$) are replaced by their per-pixel training mean in normalised space, corresponding to the mean value after denormalisation. Inversion to physical time is then performed by discrete Fourier inversion with exponential compensation on the full reconstructed spectrum.

\subsubsection{Hyperparameter Selection: $\gamma$ and $k_{\max}$}

To assess how the two key hyperparameters $\gamma$ and $k_{\max}$ affect reconstruction quality, a grid search is performed on a held-out test set using the \emph{true} Laplace coefficients (bypassing the surrogate). For each pair $(\gamma, k_{\max})$, the spectrum is truncated at $k_{\max}$ (replacing higher frequencies with their training mean) and inverted; the mean $L^2$ relative error is recorded. Results are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:gamma_opti}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{gamma_opti.png}
    \caption{Left: mean $L^2$ reconstruction error (\%) as a function of $\gamma$ and $k_{\max}$, using the true Laplace coefficients on 100 test samples. The red star marks the optimal pair. Right: error curves per $\gamma$ as a function of $k_{\max}$.}
    \label{fig:gamma_opti}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Sensitivity to $\gamma$.}
The reconstruction error is virtually insensitive to $\gamma$ in the moderate range $|\gamma| \lesssim 5 \times 10^{-3}$: the exponential window $e^{-\gamma t}$ deviates from unity by at most a few percent over the simulation horizon, leaving the Laplace coefficients nearly unchanged. This invariance breaks down at larger magnitudes ($|\gamma| = 0.02$): large positive values suppress late-time dynamics; large negative values amplify them and cause numerical blow-up during inversion. We therefore fix $\gamma = 0.0$, the natural choice in the absence of a compelling reason to shift the Bromwich contour.

\paragraph{Choice of $k_{\max}$.}
Increasing $k_{\max}$ reduces the truncation error but raises the prediction difficulty: higher-frequency Laplace fields exhibit finer spatial oscillations, making them harder to approximate accurately regardless of the surrogate method. There is therefore a trade-off between spectral completeness and per-frequency prediction quality. The spectral energy profile (Figure~\ref{fig:spectral_energy}) provides a natural guide: below $k \approx 20$, energy decreases by roughly two orders of magnitude relative to the DC component, after which the marginal gain in reconstruction fidelity does not justify the added prediction difficulty. The choice $k_{\max} = 20$ is consequently somewhat subjective, guided jointly by this energy criterion and the empirical error curves of Figure~\ref{fig:gamma_opti}.

\subsection{Latent Space Representation}

\subsubsection{Tucker POD Surrogate}

Unlike the approaches that follow, Tucker POD operates directly in the physical time domain without any Laplace transformation.

\paragraph{Algorithm.}
The Tucker POD method, introduced by Ammar \emph{et al.}~\cite{ammar2024hybrid}, computes a greedy rank-$(1,1,1)$ Tucker decomposition of the full training tensor by sequentially extracting separable modes. Given the three-dimensional tensor $\bm{H} \in \R^{n_r \times n_s \times N_t}$, the decomposition seeks an approximation:
\begin{equation}
    H_{r,s,t} \approx \sum_{j=1}^{n_f} \alpha_j\, F_{r,j}\, G_{s,j}\, P_{t,j},
    \label{eq:tucker}
\end{equation}
where $\bm{F}$, $\bm{P}$, $\bm{G}$ collect spatial, temporal, and simulation-dependent modes respectively, and $\bm{\alpha}$ the singular values. Each mode is extracted from the current residual by a rank-1 ALS problem; starting from random vectors $R, S, T$, the updates are:
\begin{equation}
    R \leftarrow \frac{\bm{R}^{(j)}_{(1)}\,(T \otimes S)}{\|S\|^2\,\|T\|^2},
    \qquad
    S \leftarrow \frac{\bm{R}^{(j)}_{(2)}\,(R \otimes T)}{\|R\|^2\,\|T\|^2},
    \qquad
    T \leftarrow \frac{\bm{R}^{(j)}_{(3)}\,(S \otimes R)}{\|R\|^2\,\|S\|^2},
    \label{eq:als}
\end{equation}
where $\bm{R}^{(j)}_{(k)}$ is the mode-$k$ unfolding. After convergence, the normalised vectors are stored, the residual is deflated, and the process repeats until the relative residual $\|\bm{R}^{(j)}\|_F / \|\bm{H}\|_F$ falls below tolerance or $n_f$ modes are extracted.

\paragraph{Application to the CH4 dataset.}
The decomposition is applied to the full training set of $n_s = 8\,100$ simulations. Because the algorithm requires the entire tensor to reside in memory simultaneously, the spatial grid is subsampled with stride $\delta = 5$, reducing each $200 \times 200$ frame to a $40 \times 40$ grid ($n_r = 1\,600$ nodes). $n_f = 300$ modes are extracted; the convergence of the relative residual as a function of mode index is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:svd}(a).

\paragraph{Surrogate training.}
The factors $\bm{F}$, $\bm{P}$, $\bm{\alpha}$ are fixed after decomposition. A four-layer MLP learns $\vtheta_{\mathrm{norm}} \mapsto \hat{\bm{g}} \in \R^{n_f}$; the field is reconstructed as $\hat{H}_{r,t} = \sum_{j} \alpha_j F_{r,j} \hat{g}_j P_{t,j}$.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The observed error conflates two sources: a $\sim$10\% Tucker representation floor (irreducible) and the MLP prediction error on top of it. Additionally, this error is evaluated on the downsampled $40\times40$ grid; Laplace-based surrogates below are evaluated at $128\times128$, so figures are \emph{not directly comparable}. A fundamental memory constraint prevents operating at full resolution: storing the full training tensor at $200\times200$ would require $\sim$193 GB of GPU memory, confining Tucker POD to the downsampled grid.

\subsubsection{SVD Surrogate in the Laplace Domain}

An alternative linear approach applies truncated SVD independently to each Laplace frequency. For frequency $k$, the real and imaginary parts of $\hat{U}_k$ across all training samples are arranged into a matrix, and the leading $k_{\mathrm{svd}}$ right singular vectors $\bm{V}^{(k,r)}$ and $\bm{V}^{(k,i)}$ are extracted:
\[
    \Re(\hat{U}_k) \approx \bm{c}^{(k,r)} \bigl(\bm{V}^{(k,r)}\bigr)^\top, \qquad
    \Im(\hat{U}_k) \approx \bm{c}^{(k,i)} \bigl(\bm{V}^{(k,i)}\bigr)^\top.
\]
The spatial basis $\bm{V}$ is fixed during training. For each frequency $k$, a dedicated three-layer MLP learns to predict the normalised coefficients $\bm{c}^{(k,r)}$ and $\bm{c}^{(k,i)}$ from the physical parameters. Field reconstruction follows by projecting the predicted coefficients onto the fixed bases; the full time series is then recovered by inverting the Laplace transform as in~\eqref{eq:laplace}.

    \caption{SVD reconstruction error vs.\ $k_{\mathrm{svd}}$ for the per-frequency SVD approach.}
    \label{fig:lapsvd}
\end{figure}

While the per-frequency SVD factorisation operates on the full $200 \times 200$ spatial resolution (circumventing the memory bottleneck of Tucker POD), it faces its own irreducible limitation: the linear basis truncation error is already substantial at the lowest frequencies (5--10\%) and grows monotonically with frequency. Consequently, the MLP surrogates are trained on noisy targets that are only approximate representations of the true Laplace fields. The combination of fixed linear bases with inherent reconstruction error and complex non-linear prediction tasks yields poor generalisation on the test set, motivating the transition to a learned, non-linear autoencoder.

\subsubsection{Autoencoder-Based Latent Space}

Both SVD approaches constrain the surrogate to a fixed linear subspace, and their frozen bases cannot be fine-tuned jointly with the downstream surrogate. Section~\ref{sec:stationary} showed that this is precisely the decisive limitation: unlocking the decoder reduced error from 27.7\% to 9.9\%. To recover that degree of freedom, a learned autoencoder extracts a non-linear latent representation shared across all frequencies; being a differentiable network, its decoder can later be fine-tuned end-to-end. The encoder and decoder are conditioned on the frequency index $k$ via a shared set of weights, allowing a single network pair to process the full spectrum. For architectural convenience, fields are bilinearly interpolated from $200\times200$ to $128\times128$ ($N=128$ being a power of two compatible with the successive upsampling steps of the decoder).

\paragraph{Sinusoidal frequency encoding.}
The frequency ratio $f = k/N_{\mathrm{half}} \in [0,1]$ is embedded via sinusoidal basis functions inspired by NeRF positional encoding \cite{mildenhall2020nerf}:
\begin{equation}
    \bm{e}(f) = \mathrm{MLP}\Bigl(\bigl[\sin(2^i\pi f),\; \cos(2^i\pi f)\bigr]_{i=0}^{L-1}\Bigr), \qquad L = 8.
    \label{eq:posenc}
\end{equation}
A plain scalar $f$ would conflate nearby frequencies and fail to capture the sharp transition in field structure between low and high frequencies. The multi-scale sinusoidal basis explicitly represents $f$ at $L$ different resolutions, providing the network with a rich, non-saturating signal that allows fine discrimination across the full frequency range.

\paragraph{FiLM conditioning.}
To share encoder/decoder weights across all frequencies while adapting to each one, the FiLM mechanism \cite{perez2018film} is employed. At each convolutional layer $\ell$, the frequency embedding $\bm{e}(f)$ is projected to a channel-wise gain $\alpha_\ell \in \R^{C_\ell}$ and bias $\delta_\ell \in \R^{C_\ell}$, modulating the activations element-wise:
\begin{equation}
    \bm{x}_\ell \;\leftarrow\; \bm{x}_\ell \odot \bigl(1 + \alpha_\ell(\bm{e}(f))\bigr) \;+\; \delta_\ell(\bm{e}(f)).
    \label{eq:film}
\end{equation}
The residual formulation $(1 + \alpha_\ell)$ initialises the modulation close to the identity, which stabilises early training. All frequencies share the same convolutional weights, and the FiLM parameters act as a lightweight per-frequency adapter at each layer, enabling a single autoencoder to process the full spectrum without per-frequency model copies.

\paragraph{Architecture and training.}
The autoencoder encoder takes a two-channel input $(\Re(\hat{U}_k), \Im(\hat{U}_k)) \in \R^{2 \times N \times N}$ representing the real and imaginary parts of a Laplace field at frequency $k$, and progressively downsamples it through strided convolutions to produce a latent code $z \in \R^{d_z}$. Each convolutional block is followed by FiLM modulation using the frequency embedding $\bm{e}(f)$. The decoder reverses this process, starting from $z$ and upsampling through transposed convolutions, also FiLM-conditioned at each layer. The decoder outputs two separate branches, one predicting $\Re(\hat{U}_k)$ and the other $\Im(\hat{U}_k)$.

Training operates on the Cartesian product of all (simulation, frequency) pairs in the dataset, minimising the reconstruction MSE:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{AE}} = \|\hat{U} - U\|^2.
    \label{eq:aeloss}
\end{equation}
To ensure diverse frequency coverage within each mini-batch, (simulation, frequency) pairs are reshuffled at the start of each epoch. Training and validation $L^2$ relative errors (Figure~\ref{fig:ae_training}) converge in lockstep to approximately 10.50\% over 100 epochs, with no measurable overfitting.

\subsection{Surrogate and Fine-Tuning}
\label{sec:finetune}

\subsubsection{Per-Frequency Surrogate \texorpdfstring{$\vtheta \to z$}{theta to z}}

Once the autoencoder is trained, the encoder is used to pre-compute latent codes $z_k = \mathrm{enc}(\hat{U}_k, f_k)$ for every (simulation, frequency) pair in the training set. For each frequency $k \leq k_{\max}$, an independent four-layer MLP with layer normalisation is then trained to regress these pre-computed codes directly: $\vtheta_{\mathrm{norm}} \mapsto z_k \in \R^{d_z}$, supervised by MSE in latent space. Training directly on pre-computed latent targets avoids any forward or backward pass through the decoder, considerably accelerating training compared to supervising on the reconstructed field. At inference time, each predicted code is decoded by the shared frozen decoder conditioned on $f_k$; unmodelled frequencies ($k > k_{\max}$) are set to their training mean, and the full spectrum is inverted to physical time via~\eqref{eq:laplace}.

Surrogates are trained sequentially one frequency at a time, producing the sawtooth pattern in Figures~\ref{fig:surrogate_training_loss}--\ref{fig:surrogate_training_l2rel}. \paragraph{Limitations: Laplace truncation and Gibbs effects.}
Despite the substantial gains from fine-tuning, a fundamental limitation arises from the spectral truncation at $k_{\max} = 20$ discussed in Section~\ref{sec:transient}. This truncation introduces \emph{Gibbs phenomenon} (a well-known oscillatory artefact that appears near sharp transitions in fields reconstructed from truncated orthogonal expansions). The high-frequency details necessary to capture sharp concentration gradients or rapid transients are lost, creating residual ripples that the surrogate cannot overcome. This truncation error manifests as a substantial prediction floor, below which further improvements require either: (i) using a larger $k_{\max}$, or (ii) applying non-spectral surrogate approaches. The residual corrector, described next, partially alleviates this limitation by learning to suppress Gibbs oscillations frame by frame.

\subsection{Post-Processing and Results}

\subsubsection{Residual Correction}

A UNet \cite{ronneberger2015unet} residual corrector is applied post hoc, frame by frame, to the surrogate output. A UNet is an encoder--decoder convolutional network in which skip connections link each encoder level directly to the corresponding decoder level, bypassing the bottleneck. This allows the decoder to combine global context from the compressed representation with fine-grained spatial detail from the encoder, enabling precise localisation of corrections—particularly suited to suppressing the spatially structured Gibbs artefacts produced by spectral truncation.

Given the surrogate prediction $U_{\mathrm{pred}}(t) \in \R^{N \times N}$, the residual correction operates as follows. The predicted field is first normalised per-frame using its own spatial statistics (mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$), then passed through a UNet that predicts a residual correction in the normalised space. The residual is rescaled back to physical space and added to the original prediction:
\begin{equation}
    U_{\mathrm{corr}} = U_{\mathrm{pred}} + \sigma(U_{\mathrm{pred}}) \cdot r\!\left(\frac{U_{\mathrm{pred}} - \mu(U_{\mathrm{pred}})}{\sigma(U_{\mathrm{pred}})}\right),
    \label{eq:correction}
\end{equation}
Here, $r(\cdot)$ is a three-level UNet with $c = 16$ base channels ($\approx$481k parameters). The final output layer is zero-initialised so that $r \equiv 0$ at the start of training, ensuring a stable initialisation from the surrogate prediction.

\subsubsection{Training the Corrector}

The corrector loss combines reconstruction accuracy with spatial smoothness. Denoting $r_{\mathrm{pred}} = U_{\mathrm{corr}} - U_{\mathrm{pred}}$ as the predicted residual and $r_{\mathrm{true}} = U_{\mathrm{true}} - U_{\mathrm{pred}}$ as the target residual, the loss function is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{corr}} = \left\|\frac{r_{\mathrm{pred}} - r_{\mathrm{true}}}{\sigma_r}\right\|^2 + \lambda_{\nabla}\left\|\frac{\nabla r_{\mathrm{pred}} - \nabla r_{\mathrm{true}}}{\sigma_r}\right\|^2,
    \label{eq:corrloss}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_r = \sigma(r_{\mathrm{true}})$ normalises for the scale of the target residual, and $\lambda_{\nabla} = 10$.

The training and validation $L^2$ relative errors over 100 epochs are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:correction_training}: both decrease from $\sim$9\% to 4.46\%, with a small train--val gap appearing from epoch 60 onward due to the sparse temporal sampling ($k_t = 20$ frames per simulation) used during training. The final test-set error distribution is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:correction_unet}.

\subsubsection{Results and Comparison}

\paragraph{Complete inference pipeline.}
The three refinement stages compose a single end-to-end inference pipeline summarised in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}. Given a parameter vector $\vtheta \in \mathbb{R}^3$, the model first normalises the input and maps it through 21 independent latent projections (one per modelled Laplace frequency) to produce latent codes $z_0, \ldots, z_{20}$. A single batched call to the shared, FiLM-conditioned decoder then reconstructs the normalised Laplace fields for all frequencies simultaneously. After denormalisation, the full spectrum is completed by Hermitian symmetry and inverted to the time domain via the discrete Laplace transform. The resulting surrogate field $U_{\mathrm{surr}}(t)$ is finally passed frame by frame through the residual UNet corrector, which suppresses Gibbs oscillations and sharpens spatial gradients, producing the corrected output $\hat{U}_{\mathrm{corr}}(t)$.

\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

This work has explored a hierarchy of latent-space strategies for neural PDE surrogates, moving from a stationary testbed to a high-dimensional transient emulation task. On the stationary convection-diffusion problem, a VAE two-stage pipeline achieves a median relative $L^2$ error of 9.9\% after end-to-end fine-tuning, only 1.8 pp above the 8.1\% autoencoder reconstruction floor. On the transient CH\textsubscript{4} dataset, the three-stage pipeline—frequency-conditioned latent surrogate, end-to-end fine-tuning, and UNet residual correction—reduces median error from 18.5\% (unfinetuned baseline) to 9.3\% after fine-tuning and to \textbf{4.46\%} after residual correction, a factor-of-four reduction at a marginal parameter overhead of only 481k weights.

Several design principles emerged consistently across both problems. First, \emph{the autoencoder is the bottleneck}: the surrogate MLP, once freed to jointly adapt the decoder, closely tracks the reconstruction floor, confirming that improving the latent representation is the highest-leverage lever. Second, \emph{end-to-end fine-tuning is indispensable}: keeping the decoder frozen yields errors two to three times higher than joint fine-tuning, regardless of the MLP capacity. Third, \emph{a physically motivated temporal decomposition enables tractable high-dimensional emulation}: working in the Laplace frequency domain reduces the transient surrogate to a collection of stationary-like problems, each amenable to a shared convolutional architecture. Finally, the \emph{residual corrector is both simple and highly effective}: by pre-computing surrogate predictions offline and training a lightweight UNet purely on correction residuals, spectral truncation artefacts (Gibbs oscillations) are largely suppressed without modifying the upstream pipeline.

The Gibbs oscillations introduced by spectral truncation are currently mitigated only by the post-hoc UNet corrector. Exploring alternative regularisation strategies applied upstream, directly in the Laplace domain, could suppress these artefacts more fundamentally and reduce the reliance on the corrector stage.The hyperparameter choices made in this work---notably $\gamma$ and $k_{\max}$---were guided by empirical grid searches and energy-based heuristics, but a more systematic exploration could improve results further. In particular, the choice of $k_{\max}$ directly controls the trade-off between spectral completeness and per-frequency prediction difficulty, and a finer sweep jointly optimising $(\gamma, k_{\max})$ against end-to-end temporal reconstruction error (rather than against the oracle Laplace inversion alone) may reveal better operating points. More broadly, deriving explicit error bounds as a function of $\gamma$, $k_{\max}$, and per-frequency surrogate error would put these empirical choices on a rigorous footing and reduce the need for costly grid searches on new applications.
